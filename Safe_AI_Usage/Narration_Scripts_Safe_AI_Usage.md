## Slide 3 — Three Core Risks for Accountants

Using AI without the right safeguards exposes our firm to three serious categories of risk.

The first is Data Privacy and Leakage. When you enter confidential client information into a consumer AI tool, that data may be stored, logged, and used to train future AI models. Once it leaves your hands — it cannot be taken back. According to LayerX's 2025 Enterprise AI and SaaS Data Security Report, AI tools have now become the number one data exfiltration channel in the enterprise — meaning more corporate data is leaking through AI tools than through any other channel.

The second risk is AI Hallucinations and Financial Errors. AI models produce plausible-sounding answers even when those answers are completely wrong. In accounting, that means incorrect tax codes, fabricated legislation, or flawed financial figures — and you carry the professional liability for every piece of advice you provide. In 2025, Deloitte Australia was required to issue a two-hundred-and-ninety-one-thousand-dollar refund after an AI tool hallucinated a government report that simply did not exist.

The third risk is Compliance and Regulatory Breach. Sharing client data with AI may breach our obligations under the Tax Practitioners Board Code, the Privacy Tax File Number Rule 2015, and the Australian Privacy Act. Disclosing a client's Tax File Number to an unauthorised third party — including a public AI platform — is a criminal offence carrying fines of up to twenty-one thousand dollars and up to two years imprisonment.

These are not theoretical risks. They are happening right now, to firms just like ours.

## Slide 4 — Risk 1: Data Privacy and Leakage

Let us look at the first risk in detail: data privacy and leakage.

When you type a prompt into a consumer AI tool — the free or standard version of ChatGPT, for example — what happens to that information? First, your conversation may be used to train the AI's future models. Second, your prompts are logged and processed on external servers that are entirely outside your control. Third, third-party data processors may access your conversations as part of quality review processes. And fourth — perhaps the most dangerous — is what we call Shadow AI: staff members using their own personal accounts bypass all of the firm's controls entirely.

This is not hypothetical. In 2023, engineers at Samsung accidentally leaked confidential source code and internal meeting notes by pasting them directly into ChatGPT. The data was stored on OpenAI's servers and could not be deleted. Samsung subsequently banned all generative AI tools from company devices.

The financial impact of uncontrolled AI use is significant. Shadow AI alone adds an average of six hundred and seventy thousand dollars to the cost of a data breach, according to IBM's 2024 research.

The types of data most at risk in our work include client Tax File Numbers, financial statements, bank account details, tax returns, and payroll records. If any of this information finds its way into a consumer AI tool — we have a serious problem.

## Slide 5 — Risk 2: AI Hallucinations and Financial Errors

The second risk is one that surprises many people: AI hallucinations.

AI models are designed to generate fluent, confident-sounding responses. The problem is they do this even when the information they are providing is completely fabricated. The AI does not know what it does not know — it simply fills in the gaps convincingly.

In an accounting context, this can look like: citing tax legislation that does not exist; providing incorrect Australian Taxation Office thresholds or rates from the wrong financial year; fabricating case law or ATO rulings; producing financial calculations that contain errors but appear correct; or generating data that looks entirely plausible but is simply made up.

The consequences are real. A Deloitte Australia engagement team used AI to research government policy in 2025. The AI hallucinated a government report — one that did not exist — leading to flawed professional advice. The firm was required to refund two hundred and ninety-one thousand dollars to the client. Their professional liability insurance did not cover the claim, because it was classified as a quality failure.

Here is the critical point: you — not the AI — are responsible for every figure, every claim, and every piece of advice you provide to clients. AI is a tool. It is not a professional. Always verify AI output independently against authoritative sources.

KPMG's Trust in AI: Global Insights report, published in April 2025 and covering forty-eight thousand respondents across forty-seven countries, found that fifty-seven percent of employees admit to making mistakes in their work as a direct result of AI-generated errors.

## Slide 6 — Risk 3: Compliance and Regulatory Obligations

The third risk area is compliance — and this is where the consequences become most serious.

As a registered tax practitioner or accountant, you are legally bound to protect the confidentiality of client information. This obligation does not pause when you use a digital tool.

Under the Tax Practitioners Board Code Item 6, registered tax agents must not disclose confidential client information to any third party — including AI platforms — without the client's consent or legal compulsion. Breach of this obligation can result in deregistration and civil penalties.

Under the Privacy Tax File Number Rule 2015, governed by the Taxation Administration Act 1953, disclosing a client's Tax File Number to an unauthorised third party is a criminal offence. Consumer AI tools are not authorised recipients of Tax File Numbers. The penalty for this offence is up to twenty-one thousand dollars and two years in prison.

Under the Australian Privacy Act 1988 and the Australian Privacy Principles — specifically APP Eleven — you must take reasonable steps to protect personal information from misuse and unauthorised access. Using a consumer AI tool with client data may constitute a breach of this obligation, with penalties for serious breaches reaching up to fifty million dollars.

Your obligations as an accountant do not stop at the office door. They apply to every digital tool you use — including AI.

## Slide 7 — Consumer vs. Enterprise AI: Know the Difference

Not all AI tools carry the same level of risk. The critical distinction is between consumer-grade tools and enterprise-grade tools — and the differences are significant.

On the consumer side — that is the free or personal versions of these tools — your prompts may be used to train future AI models, conversations are stored indefinitely, encryption is basic, there are no organisation-level admin controls, there are no compliance certifications, and there is no audit trail of what was asked or answered.

On the enterprise side — tools like ChatGPT Enterprise, Microsoft 365 Copilot, or Google Gemini for Workspace — your data is never used for training, data retention is configurable or set to zero, encryption uses the AES-256 standard at rest and in transit, there are full admin controls and single sign-on capabilities, these tools hold certifications including SOC 2 and ISO 27001, and every interaction is fully logged for audit purposes.

The bottom line is this: when it comes to client work, only enterprise-grade tools provide the protections we need. Always check with your IT team or management before using any AI tool with client information.

## Slide 8 — What You Should Never Input Into Consumer AI

This slide is straightforward — and it is one of the most important.

There are six categories of information that should never be entered into a consumer AI tool.

Client identifiers: this includes full client names, Tax File Numbers, Australian Business Numbers linked to identifiable clients, and personal details such as dates of birth or addresses.

Financial data: bank account numbers, full financial statements, tax returns containing client details, and payroll records or salary figures.

Login credentials: passwords, Personal Identification Numbers, API keys, Australian Taxation Office portal access codes, or client portal login details.

Legal and contract data: client contracts that include names, legal advice files, details of disputes or litigation, and trust deed information.

Internal firm data: staff salary information, internal strategy documents, our firm's pricing and fee structures, and any pending Australian Taxation Office audits or disputes.

Health and sensitive information: medical certificates, National Disability Insurance Scheme data, workers compensation details, personal hardship disclosures, and other sensitive personal circumstances.

If any of this information is in your prompt — stop. Either remove and anonymise it, or do not use AI for that task at all.

## Slide 9 — Safe vs. Unsafe: Practical Examples for Accountants

Let us make this concrete with four real-world examples showing the difference between unsafe and safe AI use.

Example one. Unsafe: typing "My client Jane Smith, Tax File Number four-five-six-seven-eight-nine-one-two-three, earned two hundred and fifty thousand dollars last year — what can she claim?" Safe alternative: "What deductions are available for a consultant earning between two hundred and three hundred thousand dollars with home office expenses?"

Example two. Unsafe: pasting in "Here is our client's bank statement — summarise the key transactions" with the actual statement attached. Safe alternative: "What patterns should I look for in bank statement analysis for a small retail business?"

Example three. Unsafe: "Draft an email to John Brown at ABC Proprietary Limited about his overdue Business Activity Statement lodgement." Safe alternative: "Draft a professional follow-up email to a client reminding them their Business Activity Statement is overdue."

Example four. Unsafe: "Here is the financial statement for Smith and Co — what are the major risks?" with identifiable data included. Safe alternative: "What are common financial risks for a services business with revenue of approximately two million dollars and high debtors?"

The pattern is simple. Ask AI about concepts, scenarios, and general principles. Never ask it about specific, identifiable clients or their data.

The golden rule: if you would not email it to a stranger, do not put it in a consumer AI chatbot.

## Slide 10 — Data Anonymisation: Using AI Without Exposing Client Data

So what do you do when you genuinely need AI's help with a real client scenario? The answer is anonymisation. Strip the identifying information before you ask the question.

There are three practical techniques.

Technique one is Name Masking. Before entering a scenario, replace all names, addresses, and identifying details with generic labels. Instead of "Jane Smith at 14 Collins Street, Melbourne earns one hundred and eighty thousand dollars" — you write "Client A earns one hundred and eighty thousand dollars per year." The AI gets all the context it needs, and the client's identity stays protected.

Technique two is Value Generalisation. Replace exact financial figures with approximate ranges. Instead of "Revenue: two million, three hundred and forty-seven thousand, eight hundred and ninety-one dollars — Payroll: eight hundred and ninety-one thousand, two hundred and thirty-four dollars" — you write "Revenue: approximately two-point-three million — Payroll: approximately nine hundred thousand — Profit margin: approximately nineteen percent." The question is just as useful to you — and the answer will still be relevant.

Technique three is Removing Reference Numbers. Before pasting any text into an AI tool, strip out all Tax File Numbers, Australian Business Numbers, bank account numbers, Bank State Branch codes, and reference identifiers. Replace them with a placeholder label like "reference number removed."

These three techniques allow you to get genuine value from AI while keeping client data exactly where it belongs: with us.

## Slide 11 — Best Practices: Safe AI Use for Your Team

Let us summarise the key behaviours that define safe AI use in our firm.

Here is what you should do. Use enterprise-grade tools — such as Microsoft 365 Copilot or ChatGPT Enterprise — for any work involving client matters. Anonymise all data before entering any real scenario into an AI tool. Verify every AI output against official Australian Taxation Office, Australian Securities and Investments Commission, or legislative sources before relying on it. Report any accidental data sharing to your supervisor immediately — do not wait. Complete any firm-provided AI training before using AI tools in your work. And document when and how AI was used in your work, to support audit trails.

Here is what you must not do. Never paste client financial statements, Tax File Numbers, or personal data into any chatbot. Never use your personal ChatGPT or Google Gemini account for client-related tasks. Never trust AI output without independently cross-checking it against authoritative sources. Never share AI conversations containing client data with colleagues through external channels. Never assume that your clients have consented to AI use — they have not agreed to any third-party AI involvement. And never use AI to make final professional judgements — it is a tool to support your thinking, not to replace it.

## Slide 12 — Your Action Plan

We have covered a lot of ground. Let us close with three clear, immediate actions every one of us should take.

Action one: Stop and audit now. Take a moment to review which AI tools you currently use — and whether any of them are personal or consumer-grade accounts. If you have been using a free or standard ChatGPT account, a personal Google Gemini account, or any other non-enterprise tool for work-related tasks — stop immediately and speak with your supervisor. This is not about blame. It is about getting ahead of a risk before it becomes a problem.

Action two: Switch to approved tools. Going forward, use only firm-approved, enterprise-grade AI tools for any work involving client information. Speak with your IT team about access to Microsoft 365 Copilot or ChatGPT Enterprise. These tools are designed for professional use and provide the data protections our obligations require.

Action three: When in doubt — don't. If you are ever unsure whether it is safe to use AI for a particular task, the answer is simple: don't. Ask your supervisor. Use an alternative method. The cost of a single data breach — in financial terms, in regulatory consequences, and in damage to client trust — far outweighs any efficiency gained by using an unapproved tool.

If you have any questions about AI tool usage at Dexterous Group, please contact your supervisor or our IT team before proceeding.

Thank you for your attention. Safe and responsible AI use is not just good practice — it is a professional obligation.
